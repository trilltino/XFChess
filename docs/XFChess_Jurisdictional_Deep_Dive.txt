XFCHESS — JURISDICTIONAL DEEP DIVE
Canada · Brazil · Germany · UK
Compiled from primary statutory sources (uploaded acts) + 2025/2026 enforcement landscape
═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

This document elaborates the country-by-country position for XFChess against
the actual statutory text of each jurisdiction's gambling regime. Where a
statutory section is quoted, it is taken directly from the uploaded source
files (Canada Criminal Code Part VII; UK Gambling Act 2005; Germany GlüStV
2021; Brazil Law 14,790/2023).

This is a practical risk assessment, not legal advice. The conclusions are
load-bearing on two architectural facts of XFChess. Verify both before
relying on anything below.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
THE ARCHITECTURAL SHIELD — TWO FACTS THAT DO ALL THE WORK
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Every favourable conclusion in this document rests on two facts. If either
stops being true, the analysis changes.

  FACT 1 — Non-custodial.
    Wagers flow into a program-derived escrow PDA
    (programs/xfchess-game/src/game_ix/create.rs:36). XFChess the legal
    entity does not hold a private key controlling user funds. The on-chain
    program logic — deterministic, immutable once deployed — controls the
    escrow. No human signature is required to release funds to a winner.

  FACT 2 — P2P skill contest, platform fee only.
    Winner is paid the pot directly from escrow to their wallet
    (finalize.rs:119-143). There is no house odds and no house bank.
    The only platform-side outflow is `platform_fee` (finalize.rs:101-116),
    a small, disclosed platform fee deducted from the pot before payout.
    XFChess never takes a position against the player; it never wins
    when the player loses. The platform fee is the sole revenue the
    platform takes.

These two facts together remove XFChess from the categories every gambling
statute below is built to capture: house-banked gambling, fixed-odds betting,
custodial wagering platforms, and crypto-asset service providers (custody +
exchange flavour).

The country fees in create.rs:15-21 are presently hardcoded to zero. Leave
them at zero until each jurisdiction has been signed off by local counsel.
The single global platform fee (already represented by `platform_fee` in
finalize.rs) is the only revenue taken by the platform. A country-specific
fee variance is the single most likely thing to convert XFChess from a P2P
skill platform into a "betting intermediary" in regulator eyes.

CANADA — Criminal Code Part VII
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

STATUTORY LANDSCAPE
───────────────────

Canada's gambling architecture is unusual: gambling is criminally prohibited
by federal Criminal Code Part VII, then exempted province-by-province for
specific licensed activities. There is no national gambling regulator; the
provinces (AGCO in Ontario, AGLC in Alberta, etc.) license what s.207
permits.

Three sections matter for XFChess:

  s.202(1) — Betting, pool-selling, book-making.
    Criminalises using a place to record/register bets, keeping a betting
    machine/device, engaging in book-making, etc. This is the catch-all
    that captures unlicensed betting infrastructure.

  s.204(1)(b) — Private bet exemption.
    "Sections 201 and 202 do not apply to ... a private bet between
    individuals not engaged in any way in the business of betting."
    This is the exemption XFChess most plausibly fits inside — the players
    are private individuals wagering with each other; XFChess as platform
    facilitates the contract but does not act as bookmaker.

  s.207 — Permitted lottery schemes.
    Allows provinces to "conduct and manage a lottery scheme" themselves
    (or via charity/fair operators). Crucially, s.207(4)(c) excludes from
    the s.207 lottery scheme definition any "game or proposal, scheme,
    plan ... that is operated on or through a computer, video device,
    slot machine or a dice game" — meaning computer-operated games can
    ONLY be conducted by the province itself. This is why provincial
    online casinos (PlayOLG, PlayAlberta) are state-run.

THE CHESS QUESTION
──────────────────

Canadian gaming case law (R v Ross [1968] SCC) accepts that chess is a
game of skill, not a game of chance. The Criminal Code s.197(1) defines
"game" as "a game of chance or mixed chance and skill" — but
s.204(1)(a)(i) speaks of "the winner of a lawful race, sport, game or
exercise" as something money can be staked on, treating skill contests
as a valid object of wagering.

XFChess's strongest classification under Canadian law: a skill-based
contest where private individuals stake their own funds, with the platform
as a neutral escrow-and-pairing service rather than a betting operator.

FINTRAC / MSB QUESTION
──────────────────────

The Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing Act
(PCMLTFA) requires registration as a Money Services Business if a person
"deals in virtual currency." Since 2020 this captures crypto exchanges,
custody providers, and crypto-fiat conversion services.

The pressure point for XFChess: the treasury_vault collecting
`platform_fee` (finalize.rs:101-116). FINTRAC could argue this is a
"transfer service for virtual currency" — money moves through XFChess's
infrastructure, even briefly, even as a disclosed platform fee.

The non-custodial defence is strong but not conclusive on this point.
FINTRAC has not published guidance on smart-contract gas reimbursement,
and the safer position is to register. MSB registration in Canada is:
  • Online application
  • No filing fee
  • ~5–10 business days
  • Imposes AML/KYC, recordkeeping, FINTRAC reporting obligations
It is a registration, not an authorisation — you do not need approval,
you just have to be on the list and run a compliance programme.

PROVINCIAL OVERLAY
──────────────────

Ontario's iGaming framework (AGCO + iGaming Ontario) regulates casino-style
operators offering games of chance to Ontario residents. XFChess as a
P2P skill platform is outside the iGaming Ontario scope. Same logic
applies to Alberta's Bill 48 iGaming regime launched in 2025.

What you actually need to avoid: marketing as "gambling" or "betting" in
provincial-language advertising. Use "wager," "stake," "skill contest,"
"chess prize pool." The language matters because provincial regulators
read marketing copy as evidence of self-classification.

ENFORCEMENT REALITY
───────────────────

FINTRAC's published enforcement actions over the last 5 years are
overwhelmingly aimed at unregistered crypto exchanges, ATMs, and large
money transmitters. No published action against a non-custodial gaming
dApp exists.

The practical consequences of not registering are not prosecution-driven:
  • You cannot open a Canadian business bank account without an MSB
    number once your activity touches virtual currency
  • Canadian investors will not invest without one
  • Canadian payment processors (Moneris, Stripe Canada) will refuse
    onboarding
  • Once you take revenue from Canadian users, "ignorance of the
    requirement" stops being defensible

VERDICT — CANADA
────────────────
  Banned?              No.
  Most likely class:   Private skill contest under s.204(1)(b),
                       outside s.207 lottery scheme framework.
  Required action:     FINTRAC MSB registration (recommended,
                       not strictly required pre-revenue).
  Avoid:               Marketing copy that uses "betting" or
                       "gambling" in Ontario/Alberta/BC contexts.
  Real risk:           Banking friction without MSB number, not
                       criminal prosecution.

BRAZIL — Law 14,790/2023 + Ordinance 615/2024
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

STATUTORY LANDSCAPE
───────────────────

Brazil's gambling regime was rebuilt by Law 14,790/2023 (the "Fixed-Odds
Betting Law"), which regulates a single specific category and leaves the
rest of the historic prohibition intact under Decree-Law 9,215/1946.

Critical definitions from Article 2 of Law 14,790:

  Art. 2(I)   "bet: an act by which a certain amount of money is placed at
               risk in the expectation of obtaining a prize"
  Art. 2(II)  "Fixed odds: a factor that multiplies the wagered amount,
               defining the sum to be received by the bettor in case of a
               prize, for each unit of national currency wagered"
  Art. 2(IV)  "Electronic channel: a platform ... owned or managed by the
               betting operator, that enables betting to be carried out
               exclusively through virtual means"
  Art. 2(VII) "Real sporting event: an event, competition or act that
               includes sporting competitions, tournaments, games or
               events ... whose result is unknown at the time of the bet
               and that are promoted or organized [by sports administration
               organizations]"
  Art. 2(VIII) "online game: electronic channel that enables virtual betting
               on a game in which the outcome is determined by the result of
               a random future event, based on a random generator of
               numbers, symbols, figures or objects defined in the rules
               system"
  Art. 2(X)   "betting operator: a legal entity that receives authorization
               from the Ministry of Finance to operate fixed-odds betting"

Article 3 then specifies what fixed-odds betting can be on:
  "I - real sporting events; or
   II - Virtual online gaming events."

Article 4 mandates: "Fixed-odds betting will be operated in a competitive
environment, subject to prior authorization issued by the Ministry of
Finance."

WHY XFCHESS IS OUTSIDE THIS REGIME
───────────────────────────────────

The Law captures betting operators offering fixed-odds positions against
players on (a) real sporting events organised by recognised sporting
bodies, or (b) random online games. XFChess fits neither:

  • Not "fixed-odds": there is no multiplier set by an operator. Player A
    and player B contribute equal stakes; winner takes the combined pot.
    No house odds exist.
  • Not a "real sporting event": chess between two random matched players
    on a platform is not an event "promoted or organized" by a national
    sports administration body under Law 14,597 (General Sports Law).
    Even FIDE-rated tournament chess would arguably fail this test.
  • Not an "online game" in the Art. 2(VIII) sense: the outcome is not
    determined by a random generator. It is determined by skill applied
    to a deterministic ruleset.
  • Not a "betting operator": XFChess does not receive Ministry of
    Finance authorisation, and does not need to, because it is not
    operating in the category that requires it.

THE CRYPTO BAN — ORDINANCE SPA/MF 615/2024
───────────────────────────────────────────

Ordinance 615/2024 is the operative rule banning crypto for licensed
betting. The text restricts "the operating agent" from "accepting
financial contributions through cash, payment slips, cheques, virtual
assets or other types of crypto assets ... payments or transfers from an
account that has not been previously registered by the bettor."

The ban applies to "the operating agent" — the licensed Article 6 entity.
It does not extend to:
  • Foreign platforms not seeking Brazilian authorisation
  • P2P skill contests outside Law 14,790's scope
  • Non-custodial smart contract escrow

The ban's purpose is to enforce traceability and source-of-funds checks
on Brazilian-licensed bookmakers. A non-custodial dApp is structurally
incompatible with the regulatory model that ordinance assumes, which
means it cannot fit within the ban's target category.

VIRTUAL ASSETS LAW — LAW 14,478/2022 + BCB RESOLUTIONS 519/520/521
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Brazil's separate virtual assets regime (BCB-administered) regulates
"Virtual Asset Service Providers" (VASPs), now formalised as Sociedades
Prestadoras de Serviços de Ativos Virtuais (SPSAVs) by BCB Resolutions
519, 520, and 521 of November 2025. Three regulated activity classes:
  (i) intermediation
  (ii) custody
  (iii) combined brokerage

Capital requirements: R$10.8M – R$37.2M. Compliance deadline: February
2026 for existing providers with a 9-month grace period.

XFChess does not meet the activity definition for any of these classes.
It does not intermediate exchange of virtual assets for fiat or other
virtual assets, does not custody user funds (the PDA does), and does not
operate as a brokerage. The escrow PDA holds funds during a game and
releases them deterministically; no SPSAV authorisation is engaged.

ENFORCEMENT REALITY
───────────────────

The Brazilian Public Ministry's enforcement focus through 2025 has been
on the dozens of licensed bookmakers that continued accepting crypto
after Ordinance 615/2024. Several Sky-blue-list (lista azul) interim
operators have had their applications denied for crypto-related
violations. None of these enforcement actions have targeted foreign
non-custodial dApps.

App store risk is the practical exposure. If you publish on Google Play
or App Store and Brazilian users complain to the SPA/MF (Secretariat for
Prizes and Bets), the store may remove the app. Apple in particular has
removed Solana-based gaming apps from the Brazilian store on regulator
complaint.

VERDICT — BRAZIL
────────────────
  Banned?              No.
  Most likely class:   Outside Law 14,790 scope entirely. Skill-game
                       classification protects against fixed-odds
                       betting category. Non-custodial structure
                       protects against SPSAV category.
  Required action:     Written legal opinion from a Brazilian lawyer
                       confirming skill-game classification IF you
                       generate revenue from Brazilian users.
  Avoid:               Localised Portuguese marketing that uses
                       "apostas" (bets) — use "premiação," "torneio,"
                       "competição" instead.
  Real risk:           App store removal on complaint. No prosecution
                       risk for a non-custodial dApp.

GERMANY — GlüStV 2021 + MiCA / KMAG
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

STATUTORY LANDSCAPE
───────────────────

Two parallel regimes:
  (1) State Treaty on Gambling 2021 (GlüStV 2021) — federal-state
      compact governing all gambling, administered by the Joint Gaming
      Authority of the Federal States (GGL) seated in Saxony-Anhalt
      since 1 July 2021.
  (2) MiCA Regulation (EU 2023/1114), domesticated by Germany's
      Kryptomärkteaufsichtsgesetz (KMAG), in force from 30 December
      2024, with a hard transition deadline of 31 December 2025 (Germany
      shortened the EU's standard 18-month window to 12 months).

THE FOUNDATIONAL DEFINITION — § 3(1) GlüStV
────────────────────────────────────────────

§ 3(1) is the entire game. The treaty's English translation:

  "A game of chance exists if, in the context of a game, a fee is
   charged for the acquisition of a chance to win and the decision on
   the win depends entirely or predominantly on chance."

Three cumulative conditions:
  (a) a fee is charged
  (b) for the acquisition of a chance to win
  (c) the decision on the win depends entirely or predominantly on chance

Chess fails condition (c). The German Federal Administrative Court has
held repeatedly (most recently BVerwG 8 C 17.17, 2018) that games where
skill predominates over chance are outside the gambling regime entirely.
Tournament chess is a paradigmatic skill game. The European Court of
Justice has reached the same position (Sjöberg, C-447/08).

Because § 3(1) is not met, the entire GlüStV 2021 framework — § 4
licensing requirements, § 4a-4d operator obligations, § 6a gambling
accounts, § 6c deposit limits, § 22a virtual slot machine constraints —
does not apply. XFChess is not a "game of chance," therefore is not a
"public game of chance" under § 3(2), therefore is not within the
treaty's scope under § 2.

§ 21 SPORTS BETTING AND THE EDGE CASE
──────────────────────────────────────

§ 3(1) sentence 3-5 expands the definition: "Betting against payment on
the occurrence or outcome of a future event is gambling. Sports betting
is betting at fixed odds on a future event during a sporting event, on
the outcome of a sporting event or on the outcome of segments of
sporting events. A sporting event is a sporting competition between
people according to defined rules."

This is the closest GlüStV comes to capturing XFChess. The argument
would run: "two players competing at chess is a sporting event under §
3(1); a wager on its outcome is sports betting; XFChess is therefore
a sports betting organiser requiring a § 4a permit."

The counter is structural and clean:
  • § 21(1) defines sports betting as fixed-odds combination/single bets
    on "the outcome of sports events or sections of sports events." It
    requires a bookmaker offering odds. XFChess offers no odds.
  • The players themselves are the participants — they are not betting
    on a third-party sporting event, they are themselves the event.
    This is structurally a wager between contestants, not betting on a
    contest.
  • § 21(3) sentence 2 prohibits "participants who have a direct or
    indirect influence on the outcome of a betting event" from placing
    bets on it. The XFChess model is built on exactly this — the players
    determine the outcome. If XFChess were classified as sports betting,
    every game would be a per-se violation by both participants. That
    interpretation cannot be what the legislature intended; the better
    reading is that XFChess is not within the sports-betting category.

§ 22a VIRTUAL SLOT MACHINES — NOT XFCHESS
──────────────────────────────────────────

§ 22a is the post-2021 regime for virtual slot machine games (the major
liberalisation of GlüStV 2021). Definition from § 3(1a): "Virtual slot
machine games are replicas of terrestrial slot machine games offered on
the Internet."

Chess is not a slot machine. § 22a is not engaged. Worth noting because
§ 22a(11) prohibits using "casino" or "casino games" in marketing virtual
slot offerings — this is a category XFChess does not enter at all.

MICA / KMAG — THE CRYPTO QUESTION
──────────────────────────────────

MiCA (EU 2023/1114) regulates Crypto-Asset Service Providers (CASPs).
Article 3(1)(15) defines crypto-asset services as:
  (a) custody and administration of crypto-assets on behalf of clients
  (b) operation of a trading platform for crypto-assets
  (c) exchange of crypto-assets for funds or other crypto-assets
  (d) execution of orders for crypto-assets on behalf of clients
  (e) placing of crypto-assets
  (f) reception and transmission of orders for crypto-assets
  (g) advice on crypto-assets
  (h) portfolio management
  (i) transfer services for crypto-assets on behalf of clients

The XFChess architecture does not perform (a)–(h). The closest is (i),
"transfer services for crypto-assets on behalf of clients." This is the
`platform_fee` question.

MiCA Recital 22 expressly preserves a carve-out for fully decentralised
protocols: "Where crypto-asset services are provided in a fully
decentralised manner without any intermediary, they should not fall
within the scope of this Regulation." Article 4(3) reinforces this: the
authorisation requirement applies only where there is "a legal person or
other undertaking providing crypto-asset services."

XFChess's posture: the smart contract is the asset-movement engine; the
XFChess legal entity provides a frontend, matchmaking, and a VPS that
co-signs game-state updates for ephemeral rollup performance. The legal
entity does not transfer assets — it requests the program to do so via
authorised game-state transitions, and the program does the actual
transfer through PDA-controlled signatures.

The vps_authority key is the pressure point. If BaFin scrutinised the
platform fee model, it would examine:
  • Does the vps_authority signature gate the asset transfer? (No —
    the PDA signature, derived from the program ID and seeds, performs
    the transfer.)
  • Does vps_authority have unilateral control over user funds? (No —
    it can only sign within the game-state machine the program enforces.)
  • Could vps_authority steal user funds? (No — the program logic does
    not permit a path from vps_authority to user-fund transfers outside
    the resolve_game flow.)
  • Is the platform fee a fixed, disclosed percentage of the pot,
    independent of outcome or player identity? (Yes — the fee is
    deterministic and identical for all games.)

These answers should hold for the non-custody defence to work. Audit the
finalize.rs and resolve.rs signer checks to confirm. Document this
clearly — a BaFin enquiry is unlikely but if it comes, the answer needs
to be ready in a memo, not improvised.

ENFORCEMENT REALITY
───────────────────

The GGL has spent 2024-2025 building out the licensed market (29 sports
betting, 4 online poker, 6 virtual slot machine licences issued at last
public count) and pursuing unlicensed offshore operators. The targets
are clearly identifiable: Curaçao-licensed casino operators advertising
in German, sports betting sites without § 4a permits, etc.

BaFin's MiCA enforcement throughout 2025 has been directed at exchanges,
custody providers, and stablecoin issuers — not non-custodial gaming
dApps. No published enforcement action against a smart-contract gaming
platform exists in Germany.

VERDICT — GERMANY
─────────────────
  Banned?              No.
  Most likely class:   Outside § 3(1) GlüStV definition entirely (not
                       a game of chance). Outside MiCA CASP definition
                       (no centralised crypto-asset services provided).
  Required action:     None mandatory.
                       Recommended: legal memo documenting (a) chess-
                       as-skill classification, (b) non-custodial nature
                       of PDA-controlled escrow, (c) vps_authority's
                       narrow role limited to collecting the disclosed
                       platform fee.
  Avoid:               Using "casino" or "casino games" terminology in
                       any German-language marketing. § 22a(11) is
                       triggered by marketing language even where the
                       product is structurally not a casino game.
  Real risk:           Effectively none under current regimes.

UK — Gambling Act 2005 + Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (Cryptoassets) Regulations 2026
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

STATUTORY LANDSCAPE
───────────────────

The Gambling Act 2005 is the governing instrument. Three sections do the
heavy lifting:

  s.3 — "Gambling" means gaming, betting, or participating in a lottery.
  s.6 — "Gaming" and "game of chance."
  s.9 — "Betting."
  s.33 — Offence of providing facilities for gambling without a licence.

s.6(2) — directly from the uploaded Act:

  "In this Act 'game of chance'—
    (a) includes—
      (i) a game that involves both an element of chance and an element
          of skill,
      (ii) a game that involves an element of chance that can be
           eliminated by superlative skill, and
      (iii) a game that is presented as involving an element of chance,
           but
    (b) does not include a sport."

This is the cleanest line of any of the four statutes. s.6(2)(b) carves
"a sport" out of the "game of chance" definition entirely. Chess is
treated as a sport by Sport England, the British Olympic Association
(observer status), and the International Olympic Committee (recognised
since 1999, IOC observer federation). The Gambling Commission has not
issued formal guidance that chess is "a sport" for s.6 purposes, but the
combined position of UK sporting bodies makes this the natural reading.

If chess is a sport under s.6(2)(b), it is not a "game of chance" under
s.6, therefore not "gaming" under s.6(1), therefore not "gambling" under
s.3(a).

s.9 — BETTING
─────────────

s.9 defines "betting" as "making or accepting a bet on—
  (a) the outcome of a race, competition or other event or process,
  (b) the likelihood of anything occurring or not occurring, or
  (c) whether anything is or is not true."

This is the alternative route by which XFChess might be captured. Two
players wager on the outcome of their chess game — is that "betting on
the outcome of a competition" under s.9(a)?

The defensive answer is the same structural argument as in Germany: the
players are participants in the competition, not third parties betting
on it. s.9 is built to capture bookmakers and exchange operators taking
positions on outcomes between non-participants. The Gambling Commission's
guidance on betting consistently frames it as wagering on someone else's
event.

There is also a "pool betting" carve-out in s.12 — wagering between
participants where the operator is not taking a position. This is the
closest existing concept to what XFChess does.

s.10 — LOTTERY
──────────────

A lottery requires a payment to acquire a chance, with the outcome
determined "wholly by chance" (s.14). Chess fails this entirely.

REMOTE GAMBLING AND TERRITORIAL APPLICATION
────────────────────────────────────────────

s.33 only applies to remote gambling if "facilities are used [in Great
Britain]" or "equipment is situated in Great Britain" (s.36(3)). XFChess
running on Solana validators outside Britain, with a frontend served from
non-UK CDN, falls within s.33 only by the "facilities used in Great
Britain" arm — i.e., UK residents accessing the platform.

The Gambling Commission's published position since 2017 has been that
they will pursue platforms that "target the British market." Geo-blocking
UK IPs, requiring residency declarations, and avoiding GBP advertising
all reduce the targeting argument.

FINANCIAL PROMOTIONS — THE LIVE RISK
─────────────────────────────────────

Even if XFChess is outside the Gambling Act, the FCA's financial
promotions regime under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000
(FSMA) currently captures crypto-related marketing. Since October 2023,
promoting "qualifying cryptoassets" to UK persons requires FCA approval
or a recognised exemption.

This is the one area with active teeth right now. The FCA has issued 12+
final notices to crypto promoters in 2024-2025. If XFChess runs paid UK
advertising mentioning crypto stake/winnings, it falls within scope.

Passive availability — UK users finding XFChess on their own — does not
trigger the financial promotions regime. The regime captures active
promotion: paid Google Ads, Meta ads, influencer arrangements, branded
content.

THE INCOMING REGIME — FSMA CRYPTOASSETS REGULATIONS 2026
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

On 4 February 2026, Parliament made the Financial Services and Markets
Act 2000 (Cryptoassets) Regulations 2026. Live date: 25 October 2027.
Application window opens 30 September 2026, closes 28 February 2027.

The regime brings crypto-asset services within the FCA's full regulatory
remit (not just AML). Activities requiring FCA authorisation will mirror
the MiCA categories closely: custody, exchange, trading platform
operation, transfer services, etc.

The non-custodial defence developed for MiCA will apply directly. The
UK regime is being drafted with explicit reference to MiCA's
decentralised carve-out, and the Property (Digital Assets) Bill (2024)
treats certain digital assets as personal property — strengthening the
argument that PDA-controlled funds are user property, not custodian-held.

The UK Gambling Commission opened its industry forum discussion of
allowing crypto payments for licensed gambling operators in February
2026, with the FCA regime as the foundation. This is the medium-term
opportunity: a UK-licensed pathway for crypto chess wagering, conditional
on FCA crypto authorisation, could exist from 2028 onwards.

ENFORCEMENT REALITY
───────────────────

The Gambling Commission's published enforcement actions through 2024-2025
focus on:
  • UK-licensed operators with AML failings (multi-million GBP fines
    issued to William Hill, Entain, Star Sports)
  • Unlicensed white-label sports betting operators
  • Affiliate marketers targeting UK consumers

The FCA's crypto-related enforcement focuses on:
  • Unregistered exchange operators (Binance UK, FTX UK)
  • Stablecoin issuers
  • Crypto promoters running unapproved ads

Neither has pursued a non-custodial gaming dApp.

VERDICT — UK
────────────
  Banned?              No.
  Most likely class:   Outside Gambling Act 2005 entirely under
                       s.6(2)(b) — chess as a sport. Alternative
                       defence: not "betting" under s.9 because the
                       players are participants, not third-party
                       bettors.
  Required action:     None mandatory before October 2027. After
                       FCA crypto regime goes live, monitor for
                       whether the non-custodial carve-out persists.
  Avoid:               Paid UK advertising mentioning crypto without
                       FCA promotions approval or a recognised
                       exemption. This is the one live legal risk.
  Real risk:           Financial promotions enforcement if you run
                       UK ad campaigns. Otherwise minimal.

CROSS-CUTTING CONCERNS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

THE vps_authority KEY
─────────────────────

The vps_authority appears in every country analysis as the load-bearing
question on the non-custodial defence. Across MiCA, FCA forthcoming
regime, and FINTRAC, the same test applies: does the platform entity
exercise unilateral control over user funds at any point?

Audit checklist for this key:
  □ Confirm vps_authority cannot move escrow funds outside the
    resolve_game / finalize game-state machine
  □ Confirm `platform_fee` is a fixed, disclosed percentage of the pot,
    not a variable cut or outcome-dependent charge
  □ Confirm the program code path from vps_authority signature to
    transfer instruction passes through PDA-controlled signing
  □ Document the threat model: "what if vps_authority is compromised?"
    The expected answer is "the attacker can disrupt game state
    progression but cannot drain user funds"
  □ Consider whether the vps role can be made fully on-chain over time
    (e.g., MagicBlock ephemeral rollup state finalisation moves
    on-chain at intervals)

If a regulator scrutinised the architecture and the vps_authority can
in fact unilaterally direct funds, the non-custodial defence collapses
in all four jurisdictions simultaneously. This is the single most
important compliance question for the platform.

THE COUNTRY FEES IN create.rs:15-21
────────────────────────────────────

Currently hardcoded to 0. Keep them at 0 until each jurisdiction has
sign-off. A country-specific fee converts the platform from "neutral
escrow" to "betting intermediary collecting jurisdiction-specific
revenue" — exactly the structure each statute is built to capture.

The only revenue XFChess takes is the single global platform fee
(represented by `platform_fee` in finalize.rs). There is no per-country
variance in the fee structure. Compliance overlays (KYC requirements,
age verification) are applied via frontend gating rather than on-chain
fee differences.

PAYOUT ARCHITECTURE — finalize.rs:119-143
──────────────────────────────────────────

Direct PDA-to-winner transfer is the strongest possible posture across
all four jurisdictions. The alternative pattern (PDA → platform
treasury → winner) creates a custodial moment and engages every
regulatory framework that the current direct payout avoids.

If business reasons ever require a platform-treasury hop (e.g., for
tax-withholding purposes in a future regulated jurisdiction), do not
implement it as a default. Implement it as a jurisdiction-specific
overlay that activates only where required.

MARKETING LANGUAGE — THE CHEAPEST RISK CONTROL
───────────────────────────────────────────────

Every jurisdiction reads marketing copy as evidence of self-
classification. The platform's external language should consistently
use:
  • "Wager" not "bet"
  • "Stake" not "deposit"
  • "Prize pool" not "pot"
  • "Skill contest" or "competition" not "game"
  • "Chess" not "gaming"
  • "Earnings" or "winnings" not "payouts" (in marketing copy
    specifically — in API/technical docs, normal language is fine)

This is free and removes the single easiest way for a regulator to
classify XFChess as gambling: by quoting the platform's own marketing.

KYC / AML POSTURE
─────────────────

XFChess's announced KYC provider is Didit. This is the right architecture
because it can be activated per-jurisdiction without affecting the
core platform:
  • Canada: Didit verification + FINTRAC MSB programme
  • Brazil: CPF verification + facial recognition (if ever marketing
    to Brazilian users)
  • Germany: standard identity verification, no special requirement
    if outside GlüStV scope
  • UK: standard identity verification, age check (18+)

Run Didit by default for all users above a stake threshold; below that
threshold, identity is unnecessary and adds friction. The threshold can
be set conservatively (e.g., £100 cumulative stake) and tightened
over time.
